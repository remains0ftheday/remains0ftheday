## This is my first blog post 
April is the cruellest month. And just like that, May has passed. Welcome June. 

When I was living in Korea, I went to an informal journaling club with other teachers at the rural high school I worked at. We came together because of a love for stationary. But, during these “meetings” we mostly chatted, ate dinner, and exchanged stickers for our journals. When my time in Korea came to a close, we had a final gathering: my cute deskmate Ji-eun’s Apartment Housewarming x Journal Club (일기모임) finale. [photo]
We filled out a year-in-review worksheet to reflect on the year. Ji-eun Teacher had made it bilingual for my sake. It is very sweet and I hope to complete one each year with whoever is in my life at that time. One of my goals was to begin a newsletter. As Octavia Butler says in her journals: “So be it, see to it!”
As I figure out how to write publicly, I believe it will be interesting to view these newsletters retrospectively, seeing how my Self takes shape in email. I assume the form will change as I go.

For now, I have set three rules that will set the boundaries of my writing:
* No copy and paste: I like to bring a lot of what I read into explaining how I feel or think. Joan Didion taught herself how to write by transcribing Hemingway. The copy and paste of it all dilutes it, in my opinion. Everything that I bring into this newsletter will be handtyped. 
* No artificial intelligence: No notes. Curse generative technology in personal writing. 
* No names: If I refer to my mom or family member I will say so. I will lightly pseudonymize or go by initial, as I do in my personal journal. I think this is a fun rule. 

I lived in Korea for a year and traveled to many new places before that. I feel very lucky to have collected many stories and experiences in the past 2 years out of college. While I was in Korea, I self-pityed in my inability to write as I was experiencing a new life. I cursed the cruel, consistent pace of time, but honestly had a great experience and regret very little or nothing at all. I don’t know. I guess I was talking about writing but not doing it. But, sometimes, retrospectives are more fun. I went to two retrospectives in the last couple months: Ruth Asawa in New York and Theresa Hak Kyung Cha in Berkeley. These grounded my belief in retelling after it has all happened and having the ability to curate because of my lateness. 
I am writing to you from California, where I have been home for the past week or so. Before this, I was in Hawaii visiting my sister. Before that, I was in Northern California visiting cousins. And before that, I was in New York visiting my brother. 
I don’t think I am a great writer. But, I enjoy ideas and journaling. And I have trouble keeping in touch. So, I am thinking that this newsletter/blog can be a guide for my own betterment. S has this goal of calling someone each day and giving herself a sticker as a reward. She is very smart. I hope to begin doing this in June to stride along with her very good ideas. Expect calls. 
At the moment, I am working as a ghostwriter for a political strategy firm, writing value-laden op-eds and “thought leadership,” whatever that means. I am hoping to live in the Bay Area or New York, depending on the next job I land. I have placated decisionmaking to the Gods of employment, but I wonder if this is just an avoidant tactic to soothe my indecision. I have been thinking a lot about Sylvia Plath’s fig tree analogy so I don’t waste away: 

“I saw my life branching out before me like the green fig tree in the story. From the tip of every branch, like a fat purple fig, a wonderful future beckoned and winked. 
One fig was a husband and a happy home and children, and another fig was a famous poet, and another fig was a brilliant professor, and another fig was Egee, the amazing editor, and another fig was Europe and Africa and South America, and another fig was Constantin and Socrates and Attila and a pack of other lovers with queer names and offbeat professions, and another fig was an Olympic lady crew champion, and beyond and above these figs were many more figs I couldn’t quite make out.
I saw myself sitting in the crotch of this fig tree, starving to death, just because I couldn’t make up my mind which of the figs I would choose. 
I wanted each and every one of them, but choosing one meant losing all the rest, and, as I sat there, unable to decide, the figs began to wrinkle and go black, and, one by one, they plopped to the ground at my feet.” 

Oh, the fragments of desire. Oh, the torments of choice. May decisionmaking feel less like a cage in the coming months. 
As I spend time at home, I have access to my notebooks over the past couple years. I have been reading them. I like the Oscar Wilde quote that goes, “I never travel without my diary. One should always have something sensational to read in the train.” While my teenage musings are definitely not sensational, they are an interesting read. They include mostly the chaos of my mind, but also dating woes, things I ate, places I went, lists. It is funny how I sometimes cannot recognize the person I was not long ago. This led me to think about a poem I stumbled on via Reddit (embarrassingly). But, it was Le Guin so I guess it's fine: 

Leaves, Le Guin (2018)
“Years do odd things to identity.
What does it mean to say 
I am that child in the photograph
at Kishamish in 1935? 
Might as well say I am the shadow
of a leaf of the acacia tree
felled seventy years ago
moving on the page the child reads. 
Might as well say I am the words she read
or the words I wrote in other years, 
flicker of shade and sunlight 
as the wind moves through the leaves.”

I want to include sections of what I see, hear, read, and enjoy each month, as a log for myself and offer to others. 
* Inio Asano, Solanin: The only other graphic novels I have read are Calvin and Hobbes and Watchmen. I had to Google how to read from right to left. I finished this really quickly and loved the illustrations. I haven’t had so much fun reading something in a while. Made me think that when you are 24, you are not special. We all feel the same. 
* Truman Capote, In Cold Blood: Through Wikipedia, I found out that Truman Capote and Harper Lee were childhood friends and that she joined him in investigating this book as a Research Assistant. I found it funny and sad that they ended up having a falling out because Lee liked staying home and Capote liked partying. Nonetheless, this was an engaging read that makes you empathize with criminality while maintaining a chill of horror when thinking about the cruel capabilities of humans to each other.  
* American Psycho: Learned that it is directed by a Canadian woman named Mary Harron. I thought it to be funny and topical to the finance bro trope. Murders and Executions. Mergers and Acquisitions.
